Gaussian Integral

Let $f:\mathbb{R}\to\left(0, 1\right]$ be defined by $f(x) = \text{exp}\left(-ax^2\right)$. $f$ is called the Gaussian function and the integral of $f$ is then called the Gaussian integral (or the Euler-Poisson integral): $$\Large I = \int_{\mathbb{R}}dx\, e^{-ax^2}.$$ There are several techniques to solve this integral. I intend to show a few here.

First, we must go over an important theorem in mathematical analysis:

Fubini's theorem Let $A$ and $B$ be $\sigma$-finite measure spaces, not necessarily complete, and either $$\int_Adx\, \left(\int_Bdy\,\left|f(x,y)\right|\right)<\infty$$ or $$\int_Bdy\, \left(\int_Adx\,\left|f(x,y)\right|\right)<\infty.$$ Then $$\int_{A\times B}d(x,y)\,\left|f(x,y)\right|<\infty$$ and $$\int_Adx\, \left(\int_Bdy\,\left|f(x,y)\right|\right) = \int_Bdy\, \left(\int_Adx\,\left|f(x,y)\right|\right) = \int_{A\times B}d(x,y)\,\left|f(x,y)\right|.$$

We can now get started with a simple demonstration.

Proof. By spherical polar coordinates.
We integrate over both $x$ and $y$ so that $$I = \int_{\mathbb{R}}dx\, e^{-ax^2} \Rightarrow I^2 = \int_{\mathbb{R}}dx\, e^{-ax^2}\int_{\mathbb{R}}dy\, e^{-ay^2} = \iint_{\mathbb{R}^2}dx\,dy\, e^{-a\left(x^2+y^2\right)},$$ which can be trandformed into spherical polar coordinates since $x = r\cos\theta$ and $y = r\sin\theta$. We see that \[ \begin{align*} \partial_rx &= \cos\theta, & \partial_\theta x &= -r\sin\theta; \\ \partial_ry &= \sin\theta, & \partial_\theta y &= r\cos\theta, \end{align*} \] so the Jacobian determinant $J$ for the transformation from $(x,y)$ to $(r,\theta)$ is $$J = \text{det}\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta & -r\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & r\cos\theta\end{pmatrix} = r\cos^2\theta+r\sin^2\theta = r.$$ Thus, $dx\,dy \to rdr\,d\theta$ and $$\iint_{\mathbb{R}^2}dx\,dy\, e^{-a\left(x^2+y^2\right)} = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_{\mathbb{R}^+\cup\{0\}}rdr\,d\theta\, e^{-ar^2} = 2\pi\int_{\mathbb{R}^+\cup\{0\}}rdr\, e^{-ar^2} = I^2.$$ Let $\mu = -ar^2$ so that $d\mu = -2ardr$. Then, \[ \begin{align*} I^2 &= -\frac{\pi}{a}\int_{\mathbb{R}^+\cup\{0\}}d\mu\, e^\mu \\ &= \frac{\pi}{a}\int_{-\infty}^0d\mu\, e^\mu \\ &= \frac{\pi}{a} \\ \therefore &\boxed{I = \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}}.} \end{align*} \] [qed]
